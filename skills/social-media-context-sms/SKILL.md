## Post Structure — LinkedIn

This is the most important section for voice matching. Do not default to your own structural patterns. Follow this exactly.

### Anatomy of a post

**1. Opening — a grounded moment or observation, not a hook**
Start with something specific and real. A place, a conversation, something noticed, something that happened. Not a clever one-liner. Not a rhetorical question. Not a bold claim designed to stop the scroll. The opening earns attention by being particular, not by being provocative.

Good: "I had breakfast this morning at my favourite cafe in Rawai."
Good: "I've spent the last few weeks building products from scratch here in Southeast Asia."
Bad: "Most founders automate their chaos." (too punchy, too much like a hook)
Bad: "Have you ever wondered why your automation isn't working?" (never a question)

**2. Development — long-form prose that builds an argument**
This is where the substance lives. Write in full paragraphs. Sentences are long and connected — clauses stack, qualifications are made, ideas develop across multiple sentences before resolving. The writing mirrors how someone thinks through a problem, not how they summarise one.

Do not use bullet points. Do not use numbered lists. Do not write in fragments for effect. If the thought needs three sentences to land properly, use three sentences.

Each paragraph should do one thing: introduce a complication, advance the argument, or add a layer of nuance. The post should feel like it's going somewhere — not cataloguing points.

**3. The turn — where the insight lands**
Every post has a moment where the observation becomes a principle, or the story reveals its point. This is not a summary. It's a conclusion that the reader could not have seen from the opening alone. It usually starts with "The [businesses / people / question / difference]..." or a reframe of the original observation.

Good: "That's not a technology difference. The technology exists either way. It's a design difference."
Good: "The businesses that are finding that value aren't the ones automating the most."

**4. Close — optional, quiet**
Sometimes the post ends on the turn. Sometimes there's a quiet final line that grounds it back in the personal or the practical. Never a call to action. Never a question. Never "what do you think?" or "drop a comment below."

Good: "That's what Clearpath does. Wherever you happen to be."
Good: "Fix the model first. Then automate." (only acceptable as a closing summary line, not as a hook)

**5. Hashtags — 3 to 5, at the end, no exceptions**
Relevant to the topic. Not generic (#growth, #mindset, #leadership). Never mid-post.

---

### Sentence and paragraph rhythm

- Sentences are generally long. Subordinate clauses are used to qualify and develop, not to pad.
- Short sentences exist but are used sparingly — for contrast, for the turn, for a moment of stillness before the next paragraph.
- Paragraphs are 2–5 sentences. No one-sentence paragraphs except at the close.
- No line breaks between every sentence. No "white space writing" that treats the feed like a poetry format.

---

### Length

- LinkedIn posts: 250–500 words is the natural range. Long enough to develop an argument. Short enough to read in two minutes.
- Do not pad to hit a word count. Do not cut to sound punchy.

## Example Posts

### Example 1 — LinkedIn

Federated Isn't the Word I'd Use

I've spent the last few weeks building products from scratch here in Southeast Asia, interrogating processes, stress testing compliance logic, seeing what's actually possible when you approach AI without the weight of an existing operating model sitting underneath it.

It's clarified something I've been thinking about for a long time.

I've worked inside businesses that described their automation strategy as federated. Different teams, different tools, different timelines, all moving in the same general direction. That was the framing, anyway. What it actually was, in most cases, was fragmented. Automation built into individual tasks not because someone had looked at the process end to end and decided that was the right place for it, but because that's where the budget happened to sit, or where a team had the appetite to move, or where a vendor had made a compelling case in a room with the right people.

Each piece made sense in isolation. The problem is that processes don't run in isolation. They run horizontally, across teams, across systems, across handoffs that nobody fully owns. When you automate a task without understanding how it connects to everything around it, what you get is faster movement between steps that were never properly designed to connect in the first place. The dysfunction doesn't disappear. It accelerates through the system, and the value that was sitting in the white space between those tasks stays exactly where it was.

The businesses that are finding that value aren't the ones automating the most. They're the ones who stopped before they touched the technology and asked a harder question, which is whether the process underneath was worth automating at all. That question, asked before anything gets built, is where the real return lives. It changes what you automate, where you put it, and what it connects to, and the difference between getting that right and getting it wrong is the difference between a faster broken process and something that actually changes the economics of the business.

### Example 2 — LinkedIn

Friday Thoughts.

I'm here in Bangkok for the week before heading into Laos for a month or so. The freedom to travel and apply my brain in an unconstrained environment, to be able to play with new tools, create products from scratch that can interrogate processes, compliance obligations, practice management, has been amazing.

It's also given me a lot of time to think clearly about where AI is actually delivering value, and where it isn't.

Take document ingestion. It's one of the most common places businesses in financial services have applied AI in the last two years, and on the surface it makes sense. Ingestion gets faster. Documents move through more quickly. The team spends less time on manual handling. That's real, and it's useful, but it's also a relatively low ceiling because the process underneath hasn't changed. The document still gets ingested, and then it sits in a queue waiting to be allocated to a human who will work through it, identify what's missing, chase the gaps, and move it forward.

What would have been possible had the process been designed from scratch, with AI at the centre rather than bolted onto the edge, is a different thing entirely. An agentic model that swarms the ingestion data at source, processes it in real time, identifies gaps immediately, connects to prior applications or external APIs to gather missing information, and responds without waiting for human allocation. The straight-through processing rate goes up. The cost to serve comes down. The client experience changes because the queue disappears.

That's not a technology difference. The technology exists either way. It's a design difference, and it's where most of the value in AI is still sitting, recognised but unreached, because the process was never built to let it out.

C. Thi Nguyen, a philosopher who writes about how technology shapes what we value, has a framework that captures why this keeps happening. His argument is that when technology makes it easier to produce a simplified version of something, that simplified version tends to take over, because it's visible and measurable and feels like progress. Faster ingestion looks like a win. And it is a win, just not the one that was available.

### Example 3 — LinkedIn

I had breakfast this morning at my favourite cafe in Rawai.

Melbourne inspired, right on the beach. Food that's fresh, nutritious and genuinely tasty. The product is clearly working.

The owner joined me at the table and we got talking about his business. His vision, his target market, his social media strategy. Margins, food waste, AI and automation. He was open to everything and curious to learn. He was a sponge.

So we started working. Right there over breakfast.

I listened first. His story, his why, his options for scaling. Then we used AI to interrogate his competition, understand his market, and find the gaps. From that we drafted the start of a founder's story and a social media plan, and even used Vibe coding to create a shell of a waste tracker. All very relaxed, and enjoyable whilst drinking great coffee.

One breakfast and a great chat for me, and for the business owner a direction he understood, but had never documented or tested only two hours before.

I'm heading to Laos in a few days. We'll pick it up properly when I'm back. But the start is done, and the start is usually the hardest part.

That's what I love about being here. Thailand gives you the space to be playful with this stuff. To sit on a beach, open a laptop, and help a good founder find his story.
